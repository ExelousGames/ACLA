## Definition
Rear of the car steps out before the apex, requiring countersteer or
a mid-entry lift to keep the line. The car is rotating more than the
driver asked for, exactly when stability matters most.

## Physics
Rear lateral grip is exceeded at turn-in. The classic causes are
trail-braking too aggressively (front weight pinning the front,
unloading the rear), an abrupt steering input that asks the rear for
more bite than it can deliver, or cold rear tyres. Once it starts,
the driver's catch-and-correct usually destroys the line and the
apex.

## Telemetry signature
- Yaw rate spikes ahead of steering input, often by 100–300 ms.
- Fast steering reversal (countersteer) before the apex.
- Rear slip exceeds front slip on the trajectory-balance chart at
  entry.
- Lateral G plateaus or briefly drops as the rear lets go.

## Engineer interpretation
You're rotating the car too aggressively on the brake. The rear is
telling you it can't do that and the brake together. Two fixes: ease
the trail-off earlier so the rear has grip back by turn-in, or carry
less brake into the turn so there's less load to shift. If this keeps
happening after both, talk to the engineer — that's a setup question
(rear stability, brake bias).

## Remedies
- Release the brake 5–10 m earlier; let the front bite progressively
  instead of all at once.
- Reduce peak brake pressure ~5%.
- Soften the initial steering input — one smooth motion, no jerk.
- Check rear tyre temps; a short out-lap can leave them cold.
